Part 1: Anatomy of Thoracic Outlet Syndrome

Definition: Thoracic Outlet Syndrome (TOS) refers to a group of disorders arising from compression of nerves, arteries, or veins located between the clavicle and the first rib or an additional rib.

TOS affects approximately 8% of the population and occurs 3 to 4 times more frequently in women. The average age range is 30 to 40 years; pediatric cases are rare.

  • Brachial Plexus: Network of nerves transmitting motor and sensory signals from the spinal cord to the arms and hands.
  • Subclavian Artery: Primary artery supplying oxygen-rich blood to the upper body.
  • Subclavian Vein: Main vein returning oxygen-poor blood from the upper body back to the heart.

Causes of Thoracic Outlet Syndrome

The causes are diverse and often multifactorial:

  • Anatomical factors: Congenital defects such as cervical ribs or fibrous bands that narrow the thoracic outlet space.
  • Postural factors: Poor posture like drooping shoulders or forward head position increasing compression risk.
  • Trauma: Injury or accidents causing internal changes that compress thoracic outlet structures.

Note: Cervical ribs are present in ~0.5-0.6% of the population; 50–80% bilateral, 10–20% symptomatic.

Types of Thoracic Outlet Syndrome

The syndrome is classified into three main types:

  • Neurogenic TOS: Compresses brachial plexus nerves causing neurological symptoms.
  • Venous TOS: Compression of subclavian vein causing limb swelling and blood clots.
  • Arterial TOS: Compression of subclavian artery resulting in ischemia and cold extremities.

Symptoms

Symptoms vary depending on the affected structures:

Neurogenic TOS

  • Numbness or tingling in the arm or fingers
  • Pain or aches in neck, shoulder, arm, or hand
  • Weakness or loss of muscle control

Venous TOS

  • Swelling and bluish discoloration of the arm
  • Arm fatigue and pain
  • Blood clots (thoracic outlet vein thrombosis)

Arterial TOS

  • Coldness and paleness in the hands or fingers
  • Weak or absent pulses in the affected arm
  • Arm and hand pain

Treatment Options

Physical Therapy

Physical therapy is the first line of treatment for neurogenic TOS, focusing on exercises that strengthen and stretch the shoulder muscles to relieve pressure on nerves and vessels and improve posture.

Medications

Anti-inflammatory drugs, muscle relaxants, and pain medications may be prescribed. Blood-thinning medications are used if blood clots are present.

Surgical Options

Surgical management is considered when conservative treatments fail. Thoracic outlet decompression may involve resection of ribs or fibrous bands, or vascular repair.
